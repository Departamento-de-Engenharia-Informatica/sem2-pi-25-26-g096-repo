1 Integrative Project
In this project, students, organised into teams, will analyse, design, and
develop a computer solution that allows the capture, storage, and analysis
of information relating to the income, assets, interests, and connections of
political actors, to promote transparency in the political system and bring
those who govern closer to those they govern.
Following the good practices learned throughout the degree and in particular
in Software Engineering (ESOFT), Programming Paradigms (PPROG),
Statistics (ESTAT), Discrete Mathematics (MDISC), and Laboratory-Project
II (LAPR2) courses, this project implies applying an iterative and incremental
development process. Therefore, the agile SCRUM approach must be
used to manage teamwork in each four-week SPRINT.
The software solution to be developed must be composed of a set of Java
and Python applications that must meet the requirements. To increase the
solution maintainability and follow good software development practices, the
implementation must follow a Test-Driven Development (TDD) approach.

2 Problem Statement: Description
In a democracy, the relationship between transparency and political agents
(such as MPs, ministers, and judges) is centered on the principle that those
who hold public power must be subject to public scrutiny to prevent the
abuse of that power for private gain.
The disclosure of their financial profiles, including salaries, side incomes,
and assets, serves as a critical mechanism for maintaining democratic
integrity through several key functions:
• Preventing Conflicts of Interest and Illicit Enrichment
– Asset Declarations: These are ”balance sheets” of an official’s
wealth (homes, stocks, debts) filed before, during, and after their
term. By comparing these over time, oversight bodies and journalists
can detect illicit enrichment (wealth that cannot be explained
by the official salary).
– Side Incomes: Transparency regarding ”side jobs” (consulting,
board memberships) reveals potential conflicts of interest. It
allows voters to see if a politician’s legislative actions are being
influenced by private companies or lobby groups that pay them.
• Enhancing Political Accountability and Trust
– Trust-Building: Research indicates that politicians who transparently
disclose side incomes (even high ones) are viewed as more
trustworthy and electable than those who refuse to disclose
them based on ”privacy concerns.”
– Public Oversight: Because citizens effectively pay the salaries
of political agents through taxes, transparency ensures that these
agents remain accountable to their ”employers” (the public).
• Strengthening the ”Ballot Box” Mechanism Democracy relies on informed
choice. Transparency provides the information necessary for
4
voters to make judgment calls at the polls. Without knowing a candidate’s
financial ties, voters cannot judge whether an official is serving
the public interest or the interests of private donors.
• International Standards and Best Practices
– Global Frameworks: The UN Convention against Corruption
(UNCAC) requires countries to establish legal frameworks for public
officials to declare assets and outside interests.
– Digital Transparency: Modern systems involve centralizing disclosures
in searchable, machine-readable portals to allow for
automated ”red-flagging” of suspicious wealth increases.
2.1 Transparency Portal
The Portal for Transparency, the main deliverable of the current project,
consists in an IT solution that supports the registration, evaluation, and
analysis of declarations of interests (Asset Declaration) by political actors
and the information resulting from their compilation and temporal analysis.
2.2 Asset Declarations
Declarations of Interest may be initial, submitted by political agents whenever
they begin a term of office; regular, submitted regularly (annually)
while performing a political function; and exceptional, submitted whenever
there is a significant change in the values mentioned in the declaration,
when requested by the ethics committee, or for the purpose of correcting
errors/omissions in previously submitted declarations.
Declarations of interest must include information regarding:
• professional positions, public, private, and social positions held (or previously
held), in which institutions, the nature of these institutions, the
functions performed, and the payment received;
• support and subsidies received, from which institutions and the nature
of the institution;
• assets (real estate: urban and rural) with their estimated value;
• quotas, shares, and holdings in companies, with their respective market
values.

2.3 Analysis Tools
These tools are designed to analyse the information in declarations of interest
from a temporal perspective to detect omissions, inconsistencies, and
incompatibilities.
2.4 Platform Users and Stakeholders
This platform may be used by different users, namely:
• Political Agent - someone who performs a political function, such as:
deputy, minister, councillor, mayor, or parish council president;
• Ordinary Citizen - a person who has an interest in scrutinising political
agents and who can use the Portal(platform) to report behaviour
and processes that they consider to be lacking in transparency.;
• Journalist - a person who is registered with the Journalists’ Union
and wishes to access the Transparency Portal, on the basis that it is
a fundamental tool for investigation and public scrutiny, supported by
specific rights of access to administrative information.;
• Ethics Committee - normally responsible for: i) managing and supervising
the Declarations of Interests of political actors; ii) verifying
incompatibilities and impediments: situations where the professional
or personal activity of a political actor may conflict with their mandate;
iii) verifying the Code of Conduct: application and investigation
of possible violations of the rules of conduct and civility;;
• System Administrator - person or team who manages the system
and its access privileges;
3 Minimal Viable Product (MVP)
The purpose of this project is to develop a Minimal Viable Product (MVP) iteratively
and incrementally; therefore, the work is divided into three Sprints:
• Sprint 1 – Weeks 3 to 6 – from 9/March to 12/April
• Sprint 2 – Weeks 7 to 10 – from 13/April to 17/May
• Sprint 3 – Weeks 11 to 14 – from 18/May to 14/June
6
A description of the MVP is provided for each sprint. Teams must follow
the user stories (US) provided and consider their interconnections and
respective dependencies. At the end of each Sprint, each team must meet
the specified requirements. Teams must add USs to the backlog, size them
appropriately, and distribute them among team members1.
